Home

Propelling a boat using a fixed oar as a lever is known as rowing. In modern sports, rowers race against each other in crews of two, four, or eight.

There are two variations of rowing: sweeping and sculling. When sweeping the rowers only hold one oar but in sculling they use two. Eight person crews also have a coxswain who steers and directs the crew.

History

Rowing was initially used as a mode of transportation in ancient Greece, Rome, and Egypt. The sport first began in England in the 17th/18th century with the Oxford-Cambridge boat race in 1828. The sport became very popular in England in the 19th century and was then exported to America.

Physics

Before you can begin to understand the Physics within rowing you need to be familiar with a few concepts. The first concepts are Newton's laws of motion:

  • Newton's first law states that an object will remain at constant velocity/stationary until an external force acts upon it.
  • Newton's second law states that the acceleration of an object depends on the mass pf the object and the force applied to it. This forms the equation F = ma. From this we can see that increasing the force increases the acceleration.
  • Newton's third law states that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. This means that in every interaction there is a pair of forces acting on the two interacting objects. The size of the force acting on one object is of equal magnitude to the force acting on the other object.
